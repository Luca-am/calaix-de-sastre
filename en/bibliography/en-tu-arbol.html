En tu árbol o en el mío

Jose Antonio Langarita

En tu árbol o en el mío was one of the first ethnographies I read in my freshman year, and the first that stepped outside the classic canons of anthropology I was discovering in class. Until then, ethnography felt distant: written by white men on exotic islands, observing “others” from the outside. With Langarita, for the first time I sensed that the anthropologist could be part of what they studied, speak in the first person, and let their experience become knowledge. It was my first encounter with a situated anthropology, one that claims knowledge from subjectivity and understands personal involvement as a source of understanding rather than an obstacle.

It inspires me because it breaks silences: it speaks of desire, sex, anonymity, and vulnerability without sensationalism or distance, but with respect and sensitivity. It showed me that ethnography can also be political, giving voice to invisibilised realities and questioning the hierarchies of knowledge.
